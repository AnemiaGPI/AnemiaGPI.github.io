Problem Definition

What effects does anemia have?

According to Zavaleta and Astete-Robilliard (2017), the effects of anemia are directly related to the function of the central nervous system. Iron deficiency anemia, for example, reduces the synthesis of myelin in the brain, which causes a virtually irreversible deterioration of cognitive and motor functions. In addition, it inhibits the synthesis of neurotransmitters, specifically serotonin and dopamine, which is expressed as an alteration in the behavior of the anemic person. By affecting the development of the hippocampus of the frontal cortex, anemia also negatively influences the ability to learn and memory.[2] On the other hand, Tandon, Jain and Malhotra (2018) showed that there is a direct relationship between the prevalence of anemia and perinatal and maternal mortality. In addition, they found that complications in the intrapartum and postpartum period depend on the level of maternal hemoglobin.[3]

Psychomotor development

According to Walter (2003), iron deficiency in childhood contributes to poor performance in psychomotor tests, characterized by poor fine and gross motor skills.[4] Subsequent systematic reviews and meta-analyzes (Zavaleta, Astete-Robilliard, 2017) show that there is no correlation between the improvement of motor development and the treatment for iron deficiency. Consequently, studies suggest that the effects of anemia on motor development are irreversible.[2]

Likewise, Wonke (1998) demonstrated for the first time that anemia during childhood results in fragility and distortion of the bones as the child grows.[5] This finding was affirmed by Schundel, Goretzki and Hauffa (2014) through a study that confirmed that anemia -particularly ferropenic- is a factor in the deterioration of bone health, a fact that would affect the development of the infant's motor skills.[6]

Cognitive development

According to Grantham-McGregor and Ani (2001), schoolchildren with anemia showed, among other effects, a decrease in social interest and poor school performance.[7] Also, according to another study conducted by Lozoff, Jimenez and Smith (2006) in Costa Rica, it was found that the difference in the intellectual coefficient (IQ) between students who suffered from anemia between 12 and 23 months of age and those healthy children was 25 points. Qualitatively, the scores of children with anemia throughout the study - approximately nine years - did not equal or exceed those of children who did not suffer from it.[8]

Behavioral development

According to a study carried out by Algarín, et al (2017), those children between 6 and 18 months of age had slower reaction times to stimuli and less ability to control impulsive responses.[9] This is a difficulty that, according to Mediavilla-García (2003), is directly related to attention deficit and hyperactivity[10]

Efects on pregnancy

Tandon, Jain and Malhotra (2018) estimated anemia in 20% of cases of perinatal and maternal mortality. In turn, they found that prolonged labor, fetal distress and dismemberment, as well as postpartum hemorrhage, puerperal sepsis, pulmonary thromboembolism, and subinvolution of the uterus depend on maternal levels of hemoglobin. Regarding the weight of the child at birth, homology levels were found above 11 g / dl and lower than 9 g / dl. They are associated with double the risk in newborns of gestational age.[3]

Problem Definition

How serious are these effects?

Complicaciones en el desarrollo cognitivo: En el 2007 se observa la menor tasa de asistencia escolar a educación inicial, hecho que coincide con la 4ta cifra más alta de prevalencia de anemia durante los años 2007-2008. Se puede inferir que la tendencia a disminuir de la anemia desde el año 2007 al año 2011 fue un factor que permitió el aumento de la tasa neta de asistencia escolar a educación inicial en el mismo periodo de tiempo. Respectivamente, una variación porcentual de 31,7% corresponde al aumento de la tasa de asistencia en 5.3 puntos porcentuales. Luego del 2011, se observa que la anemia aumenta ligeramente (2.1 puntos porcentuales), mientras que la tendencia al aumento de la asistencia escolar se mantiene, lo que podría significar que la influencia de la anemia en la deserción escolar se volvió indirecta, o que existe un factor que compense el aumento de la prevalencia de anemia.[11]

Complicaciones conductuales: Zavaleta y Astete-Robilliard relacionan el padecimiento de anemia en el primer año de vida y su corrección con suplementos con un pobre control inhibitorio: aquellos niños de 6, 12 o 18 meses experimentaron reacciones lentas y menor capacidad de controlar sus impulsos, lo que se puede relacionar con trastornos de déficit de atención o hiperactividad.[2]

Complicaciones en el desarrollo psicomotor: Un estudio realizado por Ramírez determinó, en primer lugar, que el nivel de desarrollo psicomotor predominante en los pacientes con anemia, de manera directamente proporcional al tiempo que se padeció de dicho trastorno sin tratamiento, fue el retraso. Se observó también que el ámbito social es el menos desarrollado, seguido del lenguaje.[12]

Complicaciones maternas y perinatales: Según Ticona, la anemia, particularmente moderada y severa- se asocia con complicaciones del embarazo y de parto de la madre, en el feto y el recién nacido. A su vez, se asocia con mayor morbilidad y mortalidad fetal y perinatal, partos prematuros (doble de riesgo que una madre gestarte sin anemia), hipertensión arterial, infecciones genitales y de heridas quirúrgicas. Se debe enfatizar en el hecho de que la anemia durante el embarazo induce a las bajas reservas de hierro en el recién nacido, lo que provoca un retraso psicomotor, y, a la vez, aumenta la mortalidad de la madre durante el parto.[13]

Problem Definition

How are we regarding the standards?

National: According to the ENDES 2017, in Peru the prevalence of anemia in infants in a range of 6 to 59 months gives a total of 34.1% which has had an increase of almost 4 points since 2011. This value can be classified according to many factors of which the main ones are type of anemia (mild, moderate or severe), area of residence (urban or rural), age groups and geographical distribution. Within the latter we have chosen three departments in particular: Puno, Loreto and Lima province.[14]

International: global anemia affects 1620 million people, which corresponds to 24.8% of the population. The highest prevalence occurs in preschool children (47.4%), and the lowest in children (12.7%). To make an international comparison, we have chosen Bolivia and Chile due to its proximity and conditions similar to our country .[15]
