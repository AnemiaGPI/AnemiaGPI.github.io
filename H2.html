Causes

In order to solve the problem we must investigate what is causing it.

We have identified two main causes, which are Inadequate intake of iron and micronutrients, and Increased morbidity (diarrhea, parasitosis, malaria, among others). Of these, other secondary causes branch out which we will develop below.

Inadequate intake of iron and micronutrients:

Non-differential diagnosis:
There are several types of anemia such as inflammation anemia, lack of vitamin B12, among others; however, the most common is iron-deficiency anemia. Due to this and the lack of information, it is often assumed that anemia is ferropenic, which can have negative consequences for the patient.
When you treat an anemic with iron supplements when he does not need them he can become truly anemic just because of an error in the diagnosis.

Increased morbidity:

Citizen participation:
So far one of the main strategies proposed by the state to reduce anemia to 19% with a view to 2021, is the realization of campaigns; however, these did not exceed 15,000 sieves, which showed the inefficiency of this method as it was approached. Nevertheless, this changed when, on October 29, 2017, a mega campaign was carried out, which was presented as a "cultural coup", in which all the resources of the Ministry of Health were mobilized, more than 600 service positions were built at the national, located in plaza, parks, markets, among other places of recurrence; health personnel came out with megaphones to summon people; They installed stalls, not only for screening and delivery of medicines, but also for nutritional tasting and counseling, and even for festive activities. Telemanagement was used for the first time, connecting at the same time to the 25 regional health directorates and managements, but each one, with its particular strategy of meeting with its population, whether it is brigadistas, health establishments open on holidays or health care tents. All this led to an unprecedented achievement, more than 154,000 children and pregnant women screened at the national level.

Access to health services:
As a result of the evolution of the different segments of the system, during 2011 the population covered by some health insurance reached 64.2% of the total, exhibiting an increasing trend as of 2004.
Coverage in rural areas exceeds coverage in urban areas; in the former it reaches 78.4% of the population, in 2011, while in the latter, it reaches 61.8%. However, within the urban area there is a different behavior between Metropolitan Lima and the rest of urban areas.

Lack of sanitation:
The data indicate that about 87.0% of the inhabitants have access to basic sanitation services. However, this indicator also shows significant variability at the regional level. In 6 of the 25 regions, the sanitation indicator reaches values above 90.0%, with Callao being the region with the highest relative value (96.8%), while the lowest values are observed in Ucayali, Pasco and Loreto , with rates of access to sanitation services of 39.7%; 55.4% and 55.9%, respectively.
